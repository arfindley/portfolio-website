SplashSync

Overview

SplashSync is a responsive web app that simplifies real-time wind, wave, and weather reports for sailors, surfers, and divers. Designed for safety and ease, it transforms complex data into clear, visual insights, helping users make informed decisions. With personalized onboarding, intuitive navigation, and safety recommendations, SplashSync ensures water sports enthusiasts can enjoy stress-free, well-prepared days on the water.

Discovering Conflict

SplashSync is a responsive web app that simplifies real-time wind, wave, and weather reports for sailors, surfers, and divers. Splashsync addresses the challenges water sports enthusiasts face with interpreting complex wind, wave, and weather data by offering an app focused on safety and simplicity. Guided by user personas, the app prioritizes real-time data, customizable dashboards, and localized weather insights to meet diverse needs. Through research and iteration, the design focuses on creating clear, intuitive displays and promoting safety habits, enabling users to make informed decisions while out on the water.

Competetive Research

To make the app more competitive in the market I had to first see what was out there and how could fill the gaps. I did this by using the SWOT method.

Lazy Surfer

Strengths

  • An individualized experience, the user can rate their session, so the preferences are geared towards the specific user rather than a general preference setting
  • It has an add friend feature so you can check out their sessions as well
  • Its fairly priced at 49.99/ year
  • The developers are actively taking suggestions from user reviews

Weaknesses

  • Navigation is a little confusing, once you open drop down navigation bar there are 4 icons at the top but its not clear what they are supposed to be until clicking on them
  • The tips and tricks takes you to a Youtube page instead of just having within the app

Opportunities

  • Better defined navigation menu would set my product apart
  • Having access to purchase or rent surfing gear would also set my app apart.
  • Having safety tips readily available

Threats

  • Because its a newer app, apps like Surfline that have been around for awhile they have a wider following and with each new update could potentially surpass Lazy Surfer

Surfline

Strengths

  • Forecasts are solid and are available as soon as you open the app
  • Has a surf shop you can buy surfing gear from which is convenient
  • Has a news feature which could come in handy for surfing outcasts and temperature forecasts

Weaknesses

  • The homepage is not aesthetically pleasing like other surfing forecast apps that I’ve seen
  • It’s a little pricey compared to other apps offering the same thing (99.99/year)
  • It doesn’t have a social feature like adding a friend or a chats

Opportunities

  • Adding in a social component like a chat area or and add friend feature
  • Having a more up to date design overall but specially with the homepage
  • Having safety tip readily available

Threats

  • Because the website has been around since 1985, the app is a step in the right direction but staying stuck in older design patterns could be an issue. I noticed this may be the case with the homepage as it seems a little rudimentary in design.

User Interviews

Now that I knew what the app was up against, I wanted to start interviewing users that use similar apps to get a deeper understanding of their needs and frustrations. I did this by interviewing six participants and asking them 16 questions through moderated remote testing

Research Goal

  1. Have a better understanding of what types of information users tend to prioritize for decision making in their water sport activities
  2. What are user’s perceptions of the trustworthiness and reliability of weather data that is currently available and how can improve upon that
  3. Understand how users interpret and apply weather data to plan for their water-based activities and identify any challenges users face in understanding meteorological information.

Summary

The user interviews revealed key insights into the specific weather information crucial for water sports enthusiasts. For surfers, the focus lies on wave height, wind speed, and swell direction, with wave conditions taking precedence in decision-making. Paddleboarders prioritize wind speed for ease of paddling and wave conditions for stability, while boaters prioritize wind speed and direction for navigation safety, with wave conditions playing a secondary role All users emphasize the importance of clear visuals and user-friendly interfaces in interpreting weather data. They commonly cross-check forecasts from reliable sources and value historical accuracy for building trust in weather apps, with specialized marine-focused platforms deemed more trustworthy than general weather apps.

User Personas

Based on the user interviews I developed user personas, like Alex and Emma, to deeply understand the unique goals and challenges of water sports enthusiasts, ensuring the app addresses real user needs such as safety, clarity, and convenience.

User Flows

Using these personas, I mapped user flows to identify key touchpoints, streamlining tasks like checking real-time conditions and navigating user profiles. This approach enabled the design of intuitive, visually clear experiences that reduce stress, promote informed decision-making, and make Splashsync an indispensable tool for users.

Usability Testing and Iterations

Through user testing and usability testing I learned that maritime professionals needed real-time, accurate weather updates tailored to their needs. They also sought a highly customizable, user-friendly app to quickly access vital information in high-pressure situations. These insights guided my design, focusing on clarity and efficiency. I tested 6 participants using an unmoderated remote testing method.

The Challenges

Balancing Industry Knowledge

Due to the target audience being experts, beginners and everything in between I found balancing the knowledge of the users to be a challenge to meet in the app. How do I make the experts feel engaged while making sure the beginners aren’t overwhelmed with new jargon and while also ensuring everyone else in the middle doesn’t feel left out?

Living in a Land Locked State

I currently reside in a landlocked state, and while I personally enjoy paddleboarding on Indiana’s lakes most of this research took place in the off seasons. This made for some challenging research. Ideally, I would have liked to be out on the water interviewing face to face while the users are in their element

The Lessons

This was my first UX case study and while I learned a lot one of the major takeaways is that sometimes simple is better. I was worried about balancing the app, so experts don’t feel disengaged, and beginners aren’t overwhelmed, but through my research I found that a lot of experts were also feeling overwhelmed with complex weather jargon. The solution was a lot less complicated than I was trying to make it. I went with a simple layout, kept the wording easy to read and to the point, and added in a glossary for extra measure. I also learned that for research, location and timing do make a difference. It’s not to say that creating an app like this in a land locked state shouldn’t be done, but you do have to get creative with not only how you choose your participants but the questions you are asking. All in all, these challenges helped me grow as a designer and I look forward to continuing this journey with SplashSync.
